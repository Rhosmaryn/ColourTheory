COLOUR
THEORY

Colour theory is the building block of effective visual communication in design and art. It encompasses the study of how colours interact, influence emotions, and guide perceptions.

For designers and artists, a solid understanding of colour theory is essential to create harmonious compositions, evoke desired feelings, and convey messages with clarity and impact. Mastering this tool is fundamental to bringing vision to life on canvas, screen, or any other medium.

Colour
Combinations

And why are they so important?

Monochromatic

Uses various shades, tones, and tints of a single colour. For example, using light peach, orange, and dark burnt orange together. This scheme evokes unity and harmony, often giving a serene and cohesive feel.

Analogous

Combines colours that are adjacent to each other on the colour wheel. For instance, pairing orange with yellow and red. It's often seen as vibrant yet harmonious, due to the close relation of the colours.

Complementary

Pairs colours opposite each other on the colour wheel. Orange and blue are complementary colours. This combination is dynamic and high-contrast, creating a bold and energetic feel.

Split-Complementary

Uses one base colour and two secondary colours adjacent to its complementary. For orange, this would mean pairing it with blue-green and blue-violet. This scheme provides contrast while retaining some of the harmony of analogous colours.

Triadic

Three evenly spaced colours around the colour wheel. Using orange as a base, a triadic scheme would include green and violet. It's vibrant and balanced, though can be more challenging to harmonize.

Tetradic

Uses two pairs of complementary colours. For orange, one could pair it with blue, as well as adding another pair like red and green. It's rich and offers a lot of contrast, but it's also the most challenging to balance.
